The AI Team Member: Enthusiastic, Error-Prone, and Off Every Invoice

Seven out of fourteen agencies surveyed by Digiday in March 2026 were absorbing AI compute costs rather than billing them. The other seven were not. The difference between the two groups is not the tools they use. It is a single commercial conversation.

Nadim A. Massih10 June 2026 · 10 min read

The brief arrives on a Tuesday morning. A fashion retailer wants a full brand relaunch: new strategy, new visual direction, new campaign suite. Twelve weeks. The agency director builds a team on paper. Strategy lead. Senior designer. Copywriter. Project manager. Four people, their rates, their hours. The proposal goes out at six that evening.

A fifth presence worked on that brief throughout the day. It researched competitor landscapes in the time it takes to make a coffee. It generated 400 concept thumbnails before noon. It drafted and re-drafted the campaign proposition fourteen times before the strategy lead settled on the seventh version. It researched, generated, rewrote, and refined across twelve weeks of production.

It is not in the proposal. That is the billing error that will define agency margins this decade.

The Most Enthusiastic Person in the Room

The billing error starts with a misunderstanding about the team.

The human team did not get smaller when AI arrived. In most agencies doing serious work, it got more capable and considerably more stretched.

Before AI tools became capable, a boutique strategy team handled three concurrent client projects. Each got the full attention of the people working on it. Strategy lead. Creative director. Copywriter. Project manager. Three projects at one hundred per cent of the team’s capacity. Three hundred per cent total directed at client work.

AI changed what was technically possible. It did not change how many hours are in a working week. The same team now runs ten concurrent workstreams. AI generates research, drafts, and creative directions fast enough to make ten simultaneous projects feel manageable on paper. But each of those projects still requires the same quality of human judgment to close the gap between what AI produces and what a client can actually use. Each project now receives roughly forty per cent of the team’s focused attention. Ten projects. Four hundred per cent demand on the same judgment pool. The team is not doing more with less. It is doing more, with the same people, in the same hours.

Time stayed finite. Volume did not.

That expanding demand sits on a resource that cannot be scaled by prompt. AI democratises knowledge. It does not democratise experience. A junior strategist with Claude Fable 5 (Anthropic’s 2026 flagship AI model) can read ten years of competitive research before a client call. They can surface frameworks and patterns faster than anyone else in the room. What they cannot generate from that research is judgment: the pattern recognition built through years of being wrong in client meetings, noticing what failed, and carrying that forward into the next brief. Experience is how judgment is built. You build it by making calls, getting some wrong, and adjusting. AI compresses the knowledge acquisition part of that process. It does not compress the experience part. A person who has been in this industry for two years with access to every strategy paper ever written still has two years of experience. The knowledge ceiling rose. The judgment ceiling did not move.

This is why the claim that AI lets one person do everything is technically true and practically irrelevant. In infinite time, any professional with AI tools could handle any volume. Time did not become infinite. A senior creative director running ten campaigns is not doing ten times the work of one running three. They are doing ten compressed versions of that work, each with less time to sit with a decision, less time to sense whether a direction is fundamentally right or merely technically acceptable. Judgment does not scale with volume. Under sustained volume pressure, it degrades.

Each person on the team is irreplaceable in a way that has real commercial consequences. The senior strategist brings not just analytical skill but a specific career of this particular client relationship: the history of what has worked, where the real commercial pressure sits, what the client will not say in a meeting but expects to see in the work. The creative director brings taste built through being wrong often enough to recognise what right looks like in practice, not in theory. The account director holds something no model can replicate: the emotional thread of a commercial relationship under deadline pressure. None of these capabilities improve when distributed across more projects. They degrade when stretched. AI is not closing in on any of them. They require years of doing this specific kind of work, with these specific kinds of people, under this specific kind of commercial pressure.

When that judgment is absent, the volume AI produces has a name. Harvard Business Review named it in September 2025: workslop. AI-generated material that masquerades as a deliverable but lacks the substance to meaningfully advance a brief. Research by BetterUp Labs and Stanford University found that 41 per cent of knowledge workers encountered it in the prior month. Each instance costs an average of almost two hours of rework. Sixty-four per cent of agencies cited it as a direct concern for 2026. The creative director is not optional overhead above the AI. They are what converts four hundred generated directions into one idea worth building. That function does not get cheaper because volume got easier to produce. It gets more critical, precisely because the volume requiring judgment grew. Three bodies of evidence arrive at the same place from different angles: BetterUp Labs and Stanford measuring the judgment deficit in weekly rework hours, Uber demonstrating what unchecked AI volume costs when human oversight cannot keep pace, and MIT researchers explaining the mechanism that connects them.

The fifth presence on every brief generates output and generates costs. Neither appears in the proposal.

This is not an agency problem. Every professional services firm using AI to do client work faces the same arithmetic. The tool names change by industry. The billing gap is identical.

“The cost is real. The invoice is blank.”

What the Invoice Doesn’t Say

Most discussions of AI billing avoid the arithmetic. This one will not.

A boutique agency. Ten staff. A three-month brand campaign.

The human team costs $72,000. That figure sits within the range of current US agency billing rates: 60 hours of strategy direction at $450 an hour, 90 hours of senior design at $280, 60 hours of copy at $220, and 30 hours of account and project management at $220.

The AI compute for the same project runs as follows. Every platform in this calculation has a subscription tier. Most professional campaigns exceed it. Zylo’s 2026 SaaS Management Index found that 78 per cent of IT leaders reported unexpected charges from consumption-based AI pricing — not outliers, the majority. ServiceNow’s CIO disclosed in 2026 that the company had burned through its entire annual Anthropic AI coding budget in the first few months of the year, joining Uber in the same admission. Deadline compression, client revision rounds, and parallel generation runs under time pressure are not exceptional conditions. They are the last two weeks of every project. Every figure below reflects that reality: a mix of API overage above the standing plan allocation, credit consumption on specialist generation tools, and subscription costs for tools procured specifically for this campaign. The total varies with the agency’s existing infrastructure. The structure is the same for everyone.

Twenty-five agentic research sessions (automated multi-step AI workflows typically running two to three hours each) through Claude Fable 5 (Anthropic’s 2026 flagship model) to produce three client-ready strategy briefs. Claude Teams covers a base monthly allocation. These sessions hit that ceiling early, not at month end. API overage above the plan allocation: $450. Copy development through Claude Sonnet 4.6 (Anthropic’s efficient workhorse model), intensive iteration running over the standard plan allocation under revision pressure: $150. Midjourney Pro for two designers across three months: agencies with standing Midjourney subscriptions pay overage above their fast-hour allocation; agencies procuring the tool for this campaign pay the full subscription plus overage. Either way, the output demanded by a brand relaunch exceeds any standard plan. Three months at $96 per user plus fast-hour overage on heavy production periods: $576.

Then the video stack, where the numbers become inescapable. Sora 2 Pro (OpenAI’s video generation platform) charges $0.70 per second at 1080p. A brand relaunch needs a brand film and a full social suite. AI video is probabilistic: roughly a third of outputs fail professional quality review, clients request revision rounds, and parallel generation runs under deadline are standard. 1,600 seconds of generated footage to deliver 400 seconds of finished film and social cuts: $1,120. Social format adaptation through Runway Gen-4 (AI video editing software): $225. Motion content through Seedance 2.0 (ByteDance’s video generation platform): $44. Campaign imagery through Nano Banana Pro (Higgsfield’s image generation platform), 500 images: $100. Eight voiceover prototype scripts through ElevenLabs (AI audio generation platform), including the redos that always run in the final week: $30. Strategy presentation development and client deck iteration, running Claude Sonnet 4.6 across three months of brief-writing, proposal work, and senior review cycles: $305.

Total project-specific AI compute: $3,000. Every dollar of that ran on behalf of this client.

But the $3,000 covers only the project-specific half. During the same three months, the agency’s standing AI subscriptions are running in parallel: Claude Teams for all ten staff at $300 a month, Microsoft Copilot Business for five seats at $90 a month, and ChatGPT Plus plans for three staff at $60 a month. That is $450 a month in standing AI subscriptions, $1,350 across the campaign period. This is overhead. It belongs in the rate card, not this proposal. It will not appear on any invoice. But it is leaving the agency’s account regardless, every month of delivery.

Total AI spend this quarter: $4,350. On a $75,000 engagement, that is 5.8 per cent of project value. Zero lines on the invoice.

The Boutique Arithmetic

Run five concurrent projects, the normal operating state for a ten-person boutique, and the monthly unrecovered AI compute reaches $5,000. Not in a crisis month. That is the standard month.

But the project compute is not the only number the proposal avoids.

Before AI tools arrived, a ten-person agency ran around $630 a month in SaaS overhead. Adobe Creative Cloud (the industry-standard design and production suite) at roughly $60 per seat for design staff. Figma (the dominant interface and brand design platform) at $25 per seat. Project management, communication, and cloud storage across the team: another $30 per seat.

AI tools did not replace any of that stack. Adobe is still running. Figma is still the environment where finished work lives. Type-setting, print preparation, precise production delivery, and the hundred tasks that AI generation cannot handle still require the established tools. The AI tools arrived on top: Claude Teams at $30 per user per month, Microsoft Copilot (AI productivity assistant) at $18 to $30 per seat depending on plan tier, ChatGPT Plus plans, specialist generation tool subscriptions. The same agency now runs $1,400 a month in SaaS overhead before a single project-specific token is consumed. Software costs more than doubled. Neither the existing stack nor the new layer got cheaper. Both are being absorbed. Neither is being billed. This is the double overhead that agency finances do not show: the legacy tools still running, and the AI layer stacked directly on top of them.

AI cannot one-shot commercial-grade work. In practice, it cannot thousand-shot it unaided. What AI generation produces is raw material: probabilistically useful, occasionally excellent, never finished to client standard without human judgment at every layer. The team exists to close the gap between what AI outputs and what a client can use. At every iteration of that gap-closing, costs are running on the firm’s account.

Two forces push the spend beyond what base rates suggest. The first: clients now know their agency has AI. They expect more output in less time, at no extra cost. That compression multiplies the generation volume required and reduces the curation time available to filter it. The second follows directly. When generation outpaces curation, workslop accumulates. BetterUp Labs put the aggregate rework cost at one hour and fifty-six minutes per knowledge worker per week. In professional services, that rework runs on the firm’s compute.

The meter does not stop because the output was not good enough.

Enterprise software teams discovered this faster than creative services because they instrument their costs. Uber gave thousands of engineers access to Claude Code (Anthropic’s AI coding tool) in December 2025. By April 2026, the company had consumed its entire annual AI budget in four months, with per-developer daily spending far exceeding projections. Microsoft cancelled the majority of its Claude Code licences across engineering teams shortly after rollout, shifting to lower-cost alternatives to contain the bill. Bryan Catanzaro, Vice President of Applied Deep Learning Research at Nvidia, summarised the pattern in early 2026: “For my team, the cost of compute is far beyond the costs of the employees.” Professional services firms face an identical dynamic. The difference is that software companies measure it.

Scale that to a 25-person agency. Fifteen active projects simultaneously. Sixty completions per year.

At boutique level, that is $150,000 a year in unrecovered project AI compute, before the SaaS overhead also sitting unclaimed. Against a typical 20 per cent margin on $3.75 million in fees, it is the profit equivalent of three full client accounts left unbilled every year. Still wrong to absorb. No longer easy to dismiss.

But a 25-person agency does not run boutique-level AI. It runs agentic pipelines. Research-heavy strategy engagements involve not fifteen Claude sessions but a hundred. A multi-channel campaign produces not 1,500 Midjourney images but 8,000. A video content project runs not a brand campaign suite but twelve minutes of footage across a full content library. At every point, the three cost drivers compound: probabilistic generation failures, client revision cycles, and the workslop rework loop the human team must close before anything reaches a client.

At this level, per-project AI compute runs $3,000 on a standard multi-channel campaign. On a complex engagement with full video production, parallel agentic pipelines, and compressed client deadlines, the figure reaches $8,000 to $15,000.

At $4,800 average across sixty projects per year: $288,000 in unrecovered AI costs. Against a 20 per cent target margin on a $7.2 million agency, that is 20 per cent of annual target profit absorbed without a single client conversation. No invoice line. No commercial argument. Just silence.

The Objection Worth Answering

The most common objection to billing AI is a familiar one.

Law firms billed clients for Westlaw (a legal research database) per search and per page throughout the 1990s. When Westlaw moved to flat-rate annual subscriptions in the early 2000s, the per-search billing stopped. Firms absorbed it. Clients stopped seeing it. The argument goes: AI tools will go the same way.

This argument is half right. The half it gets wrong is the important half.

There are two types of AI cost running simultaneously in every agency, and the industry treats them as one.

Two Types of AI Cost

Type one: flat-rate subscriptions. A Claude Teams licence at $30 per user per month. A Microsoft Copilot (AI productivity assistant) seat at $18 to $30 depending on plan tier. A ChatGPT Plus plan at $20. These costs do not change based on how complex any individual client’s project is. They are infrastructure, exactly like the Westlaw annual subscription. They belong in the agency’s overhead, distributed across the rate card. No one should itemise a Slack subscription in a client proposal.

Type two: project-specific consumption. When a strategy team runs a four-hour agentic research session on Claude Fable 5 for a named client’s brief, that compute was consumed on behalf of that client. When a design team generates 3,000 Midjourney images for a specific rebrand, those image credits are gone and attributable. When a production team renders eight minutes of Sora video for a campaign, the per-second cost is exact and traceable.

Type two is not Westlaw after the subscription. It is Westlaw before the subscription: real, variable, per-use costs that belong in the client budget.

Every agency absorbing Type two costs inside flat overhead is solving for the wrong category.

This Question Has Been Answered Before

Every generation of professional services has had to work out where its intelligence tools belong on the invoice. The answer has always been the same.

A film production company renting cinema-grade cameras does not absorb the rental fee into the director of photography’s day rate. The director of photography is the value. The camera is the cost of doing work to that standard. The rental is a production line item.

The tool is not the talent. The tool does not replace the talent, and it does not reduce what the talent is worth. A director of photography with a cinema-grade camera is worth more to a production than one without it, not less. The equipment gives them the technical range to express what they see. The camera amplifies the director’s vision; it does not substitute for it. Nobody argues that outstanding camera equipment makes a cinematographer cheaper to hire. Nobody argues that a production needed fewer people because the kit was excellent. And the rental cost belongs in the client budget because it was consumed on behalf of that client’s production.

AI model costs in agency work are the same question in a new form. The strategist is not the AI. The creative director is not the AI. The AI gives them tools to deliver work that is faster, broader in scope, and more thoroughly researched than was possible without them. The cost of those tools on a specific project belongs in the proposal, alongside the people who used them.

Seven Out of Fourteen

In March 2026, Digiday (the advertising industry trade publication) asked fourteen agencies how they handle AI model costs. Seven were passing them through in some form.

Merge (a full-service digital agency) meters AI costs and passes them to clients case by case. Big Spaceship (a digital creative agency) treats AI compute as a production cost, equivalent to motion graphics rendering or photography. Lerma/ (an independent creative agency) shows it as a line item at cost, no different in format from other project expenses. Pencil, owned by Brandtech (a global digital services group), charges generation credits covering each image, output, or second of video produced.

Seven were absorbing everything, and mistaking silence for safety.

Chris Neff, chief AI officer at Anomaly (a creative and communications agency), named the fear plainly: “It feels like a money grab.”

That fear is the real blocker. It deserves a direct answer.

“The client is not being asked to pay more than before. The client is being shown, perhaps for the first time, what their brief actually required.”

Why It’s Not a Money Grab

The objection almost every agency fears goes like this: AI was supposed to make projects cheaper and faster. Why are you billing for it separately?

The answer is arithmetic.

A four-hour agentic research session on Claude Fable 5 costs roughly $30–$50 in compute at current platform pricing. The equivalent work in junior researcher hours, billed at standard agency rates, runs around $450. The client is not being asked to pay more than before. The client is being shown, perhaps for the first time, what their brief actually required, and at what fraction of what it would have cost without AI.

That is not a money grab. That is transparency about value.

The objection also carries a hidden assumption: that AI doing more research volume means the strategist’s judgment is worth less. It does not. Volume and judgment are not the same thing. AI produces volume: research breadth, concept quantity, draft iterations. The strategist produces judgment: which angle to pursue, which brief to challenge before answering it, which direction contains the idea that is genuinely right rather than merely competent. That judgment is what the client is buying. It is not priced per hour of research reading. It is priced per career of knowing what to do with what you find.

A second objection is practical rather than philosophical: billing for AI creates a competitive disadvantage. The agency that absorbs the cost looks cheaper in the pitch. The answer is that the conversation is not primarily about price. The agencies already passing AI costs through are not losing clients over a thirty-dollar line item. They are having a better commercial relationship with those clients, because they have been honest about what the brief required and what it cost to answer properly. Clients who understand the difference between AI subscription overhead and project-specific compute do not leave over transparency. They stay because of it.

Ruben Schreurs, chief executive of Ebiquity (a global media investment firm), told Digiday in March 2026 that clients would begin auditing agency AI token usage “just as they audit media spending now, within months and years.” That audit is coming regardless of whether agencies choose transparency today. The agencies that have built the conversation into their proposals will not be surprised when it arrives. They will also be better positioned for what that conversation becomes.

The Cost of Waiting

The 2027 number is the one that ends the debate.

In April 2026, researchers at MIT CSAIL, Microsoft Research, Stanford University, and the University of Michigan published the most rigorous study yet of what AI actually costs at enterprise scale (arXiv:2604.22750). Their finding: agentic workflows (multi-step AI tasks that run autonomously across research, drafting, and iteration) consume between 530 and 1,000 times more tokens than single-turn queries. A junior designer asking Claude to generate one image is one cost. A campaign brief running autonomous research, multi-round strategy development, image generation, and copy iteration is a different order of magnitude entirely. The billing conversation is not about what a single prompt costs. It is about what a campaign costs.

The standard objection is that token prices are falling fast enough to make the whole conversation irrelevant. Commodity AI inference (standard, lower-cost AI processing) will approach near-zero for routine tasks within 18 months. This is real and deserves a direct answer.

Per-token cost is falling. Per-task cost is not.

Goldman Sachs projects 24 times growth in total token consumption by 2030. Enterprise AI bills tripled last year even as per-million-token costs fell 75 per cent. Falling unit prices multiplied by exploding consumption volumes produce rising totals. The boutique project that costs $3,000 in AI compute today carries a meaningfully larger bill in two years at lower unit rates. Add the client expectation compression: clients now expect ten times the output in half the timeline, which forces higher generation volume, more parallel runs, more revision cycles. At that rate, a conservative projection puts the same boutique three-month campaign at $4,500 to $5,500 in AI compute by 2027. Not because tools get more expensive. Because the dominant cost drivers in creative agency work, video generation seconds and long agentic research sessions, deflate more slowly than commodity text inference, while client expectations for video volume and output quality continue to compound. For a 25-person mid-size agency, the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta’s May 2026 benchmark of $289 per professional per month implies a 2028 run rate of $170,000 to $195,000 in annual AI compute, once agentic pipelines become standard operating procedure. None of that changes the billing argument. It makes it more urgent.

But even if commodity inference does approach zero, the billing principle does not collapse. It evolves.

Three Phases of AI Billing

Right now, the model is cost passthrough: AI compute as a transparent line item in proposals, at cost, the same way production expenses appear today.

By 2027, it becomes capability packaging. Globant (a global IT and digital services company) launched exactly this model in June 2025. AI Pods, at approximately $20,000 per month, bundles 100 million AI tokens with dedicated human experts who direct and supervise the AI work. The human team is not replaced. The AI capability is added. The combined offering is priced and sold as a package.

By 2029, it becomes outcome pricing: premium rates that embed AI capability in the value of the deliverable, without separating out the compute. The rate reflects the capability. But that rate only holds if the market already understands why AI-augmented work is worth more. That understanding is built through transparency now, not through a silence that makes AI look like it arrived for free.

The agencies that skip the first stage do not reach the third stage with stronger margins. They reach it having absorbed four years of AI compute silently, having established no commercial precedent with clients, and having trained the market to expect AI capacity as a free inclusion. The rate reflects the capability only if the market was taught to associate the two. That teaching starts now.

Four Things to Do Before the Next Proposal Goes Out

Separate your AI costs by type. Flat-rate subscriptions are overhead. They belong in your rate, not your proposal. Consumption-based production costs (Midjourney image runs, Sora generations, long agentic research sessions) are project costs. They belong in the brief. Know the difference before you build the next proposal.

Track per-project AI spend for 90 days. You cannot bill what you do not measure. Most agencies have no visibility on which AI costs are project-attributable and which are infrastructure. Spend 90 days building that picture before making any billing decisions.

Frame it as production transparency, not a surcharge. “AI production costs” belongs in the same section as software licences, photography, and motion graphics rendering. It is the cost of the tools your team used to produce the work. That is a different conversation from adding a new fee.

Start with one client you trust. Not a new pitch. Not every project. One existing relationship where you can have an honest conversation about what your team deploys and what it costs. The agencies already doing this are not losing that client. They are having a better commercial relationship with them.

The fifth team member was on the Tuesday morning brief. It will be on Monday’s brief. It will be on every brief next year. The only thing that can change is the proposal.
